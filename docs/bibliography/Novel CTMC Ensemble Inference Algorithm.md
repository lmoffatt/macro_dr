# **Differentiable Construction of the Symmetric-Subspace Propagator with Boundary-Conditioned Moments for Independent-Particle CTMC Ensembles**

## **1\. Theoretical Architecture and Algorithmic Context**

The computational modeling of identical, non-interacting particles evolving under stochastic dynamics is a foundational requirement across multiple scientific domains, including biophysics, physical chemistry, quantum mechanics, and population ecology. The mathematical framework under evaluation assumes an ensemble of $N$ independent and identical particles, where each particle evolves according to a Continuous-Time Markov Chain (CTMC) defined over $S$ discrete states. The transition dynamics for every individual particle are governed by a shared, time-homogeneous $S \\times S$ generator matrix $Q$. Because the particles are indistinguishable, the joint configuration of the entire $N$-particle ensemble is completely specified by a multinomial count vector $\\mathbf{n} \= (n\_1, \\dots, n\_S)$, where the sum of the counts $\\sum n\_s \= N$. Geometrically, this constraint projects the massively dimensioned labeled state space $S^N$ down to the symmetric subspace, which possesses a significantly reduced dimensionality denoted by $M \= \\binom{N+S-1}{S-1}$.  
For the purpose of executing exact, likelihood-based Bayesian inference—particularly within Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) where experimental observations consist of noisy, time-averaged integrals of a state-dependent observable $g(s)$—the algorithm must compute specific quantities over a fixed discrete time interval $\\Delta t$. The required quantities are the boundary-conditioned moments. At the single-particle level, these are defined as:

1. The transition probability matrix: $m\_{ij} \=\_{ij}$.  
2. The conditional mean of the integrated observable: $\\mu\_{ij} \= \\mathbb{E}$.  
3. The conditional variance of the integrated observable in the centered convention: $\\sigma^2\_{ij} \= \\text{Var}$.

When scaled to the macroscopic $N$-particle symmetric subspace, these objects must be indexed by pairs of multinomial count vectors $(\\mathbf{n}, \\mathbf{n}')$. Historically, deriving these macroscopic boundary-conditioned moments has forced researchers into a computational dichotomy. The first standard approach is macroscopic moment closure, which propagates only the unconditional mean and covariance of the state vector using a Gaussian approximation. This approach severely loses information and breaks down in the regime of small $N$ where discrete, non-Gaussian stochastic fluctuations dominate. The second standard approach is the direct analytical construction of the $M$-dimensional microscopic generator, $Q\_{\\text{micro}}$, followed by the computation of the full matrix exponential $\\exp(Q\_{\\text{micro}} \\Delta t)$. While exact, this requires the factorization of an $M \\times M$ non-symmetric matrix.  
The proposed numerical algorithm completely circumvents the explicit construction and exponentiation of $Q\_{\\text{micro}}$. The algorithm exploits the structural independence of the particles, recognizing that the total generator of the labeled system is a Kronecker sum, $Q\_{\\text{total}} \= Q \\oplus Q \\oplus \\dots \\oplus Q$, which dictates that the corresponding propagator is a Kronecker product, $\\exp(Q\_{\\text{total}} \\Delta t) \= P(\\Delta t) \\otimes \\dots \\otimes P(\\Delta t)$. The algorithm constructs the symmetric-subspace propagator via a binary-doubling recursive logic. Two $N/2$-particle propagators are combined using a multinomial convolution that accounts for all valid combinations of how the $N$ indistinguishable particles partition between the two subgroups. This process is iterated $\\log\_2(N)$ times.  
Crucially, this recursive multinomial convolution extends to the conditional moments through precise linear-additive combination rules at each stage:

* Transition probability: $m\_{\\text{new}} \= m\_L \\otimes m\_R$  
* Conditional mean: $\\mu\_{\\text{new}} \= \\mu\_L \\otimes m\_R \+ m\_L \\otimes \\mu\_R$  
* Conditional variance: $\\sigma^2\_{\\text{new}} \= \\sigma^2\_L \\otimes m\_R \+ m\_L \\otimes \\sigma^2\_R$

The fundamental analytical enabler of this composition is the observation of variance additivity under conditional independence given the boundary states. Because the particles are independent and the formulation conditions precisely on the start-end partitions, the cross-terms in the second moment vanish entirely. This mandates propagating the centered variance rather than the raw second moment.  
While the asymptotic computational complexity scales as $\\mathcal{O}((N/2)^4)$ for $S=2$ (which is strictly slower than the $\\mathcal{O}(M^3)$ scaling of direct eigendecomposition), the unparalleled advantage of the binary-doubling construction lies in its analytical differentiability. The entirety of the $N$-particle moment bundle is assembled purely through nested additions and multiplications of the single-particle base objects. Consequently, computing derivatives with respect to underlying kinetic parameters via forward-mode automatic differentiation (AD) is analytically exact, trivial to trace, and absolutely numerically stable. This stands in stark contrast to differentiating $\\exp(Q\_{\\text{micro}} \\Delta t)$ via spectral eigendecomposition, which suffers from severe numerical instability when the kinetic generator approaches degenerate eigenvalues. This algorithm essentially unlocks derivative-based microscopic inference (e.g., Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, Fisher Information evaluations, and score-based gradient descents) for intermediate experimental $N$ values where macroscopic approximations fail.  
The following sections systematically review the literature across six adjacent fields to ascertain the novelty, precedence, and distinctiveness of this specific algorithmic formulation.

## **2\. Ion Channel and Patch-Clamp Methods Literature**

The most prominent historical application of exact independent-particle CTMC inference resides in the electrophysiology of ion channels, specifically within single-channel recording and macroscopic patch-clamp analysis.

### **2.1 The Colquhoun–Hawkes Burst Theory Lineage**

The mathematical formalization of boundary-conditioned moments for integrated observables was pioneered by Colquhoun and Hawkes in the early 1980s.1 Their foundational treatises addressed the stochastic properties of single ion channels, formulating the underlying continuous-time Markov models that drive gating mechanics. Hawkes, Jalali, and Colquhoun advanced this framework to compute exact asymptotic distributions of apparent open times and total integrated current when brief switching events are obscured by low-pass filtering limits.1  
The primary mathematical mechanism utilized in this classical literature relies heavily on Laplace transforms evaluated over the partitioned sub-matrices of the generator $Q$. For example, the Markov chain embedded at the event points is utilized to compute the specific distributions of "bursts" of openings.1 However, the entirety of the Colquhoun-Hawkes lineage is derived explicitly for the single-channel case ($N=1$). When physical patches contain multiple indistinguishable channels, the historical analytical response has been to treat the aggregate current distributions as raw mathematical convolutions of the single-channel density functions, rather than constructing exact parametric symmetric-subspace propagators capable of yielding the discrete likelihood of the ensemble.3

### **2.2 The Milescu and QuB Software Lineage**

The problem of extracting kinetic parameters from records containing $N \> 1$ interacting or identical channels was systematically tackled by the developers of the QuB software architecture, heavily represented by the work of Milescu, Akk, and Sachs.6 To handle multiple indistinguishable channels, Milescu (2005) established the Maximum Interval Likelihood (MIL) and macroscopic rate optimizer techniques.6  
Milescu explicitly defined the boundary between "macroscopic" and "microscopic" modeling of ion channel populations.12 The macroscopic approach relies on moment closure; it approximates the average behavior of a channel population as a continuous variable governed by the mean and variance, essentially utilizing Gaussian approximations to compute likelihoods.12 The literature explicitly acknowledges that this macroscopic likelihood significantly underestimates parameters and diverges from reality as the number of channels decreases.12  
To compute the exact likelihood for indistinguishable multi-channel patches, Milescu introduced the "microscopic-recursive algorithm".12 This algorithm operates exactly in the $M$-dimensional symmetric subspace (the multinomial state space). However, its mechanism of action is brute force. The initial prior state probability vector is multiplied iteratively against $Q\_m$, the fully instantiated transition-rate probability matrix of dimensions $M \\times M$.12 The probabilities are updated iteratively over the time step. At no point does the QuB architecture utilize a binary-doubling recursive convolution of the $N=1$ propagator to bypass the instantiation and exponentiation of the $M \\times M$ generator.6  
Furthermore, parameter optimization within QuB is primarily driven by numerical techniques, standard Newton-Raphson methods using analytic derivatives of single-channel matrices, or penalty-constrained numerical optimization approximations.6 It does not deploy a differentiable computational graph built from recursive multinomial addition intended for deep-learning-style automatic differentiation.14

### **2.3 Bayesian Micro-State Inference**

Recent developments in Bayesian inference for ion channels by researchers such as Siekmann, Sneyd, and Crampin generally focus on utilizing Hidden Markov Models with Kalman filtering variants to evaluate likelihoods.17 The standard Extended Kalman Filter formulation propagates the first two moments (mean and covariance matrix) of the probability distribution of the channel states, yielding optimality only under linear Gaussian assumptions.17 Other EM-based approaches (e.g., Roberts and Ephraim) execute exact likelihood evaluations for independent CTMCs but compute the full continuous-time trajectory expectations via direct matrix exponentiation techniques.20 None report utilizing a factorized symmetric composition to achieve differentiable moments.

## **3\. Stochastic Chemical Kinetics and the Chemical Master Equation**

Within the domain of systems biology and chemical kinetics, systems are modeled exactly by the Chemical Master Equation (CME), which dictates the time evolution of the probability distribution of discrete molecular populations.23 For networks where total molecular counts are conserved (e.g., independent transitions between conformational states), the state space is explicitly the multinomial space of dimension $M$ discussed in the target algorithm.

### **3.1 The Finite State Projection Algorithm**

The most rigorous standard for solving the CME without reliance on stochastic Monte Carlo sampling (such as Gillespie's $\\tau$-leaping) is the Finite State Projection (FSP) algorithm introduced by Munsky and Khammash in 2006\.23 The FSP operates by truncating the infinite discrete state space to a finite subset of most-probable states, bounding the analytical error of the probability vector, and directly exponentiating the truncated generator matrix.23  
For systems corresponding to $N$ independent molecules transitioning between $S$ states, the FSP does not truncate; it maps the full $M$-dimensional multinomial space and yields an exact analytical solution.23 Modern iterations of FSP theory extensively utilize it to compute parametric sensitivities, allowing for the direct evaluation of the Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) to optimize single-cell experimental design.24 To do this, the FSP must evaluate derivatives of the transition probability matrix with respect to kinetic parameters.  
However, FSP strictly executes this via direct matrix exponential derivatives. It treats the generator $Q\_{\\text{micro}}$ holistically, deploying Krylov subspace methods or Padé approximations to compute $\\exp(Q\_{\\text{micro}} \\Delta t)$.23 It possesses no structural recognition that the generator for non-interacting molecules is fundamentally a Kronecker sum, and it does not utilize binary-doubling convolutions of the $N=1$ single-particle propagator to assemble the macroscopic probability vector. Furthermore, FSP is designed to propagate the unconditional marginal probability vector over time; it does not compute the boundary-conditioned conditional mean and conditional variance of a path-integrated observable required for HMM filtering.

### **3.2 Moment-Closure Techniques**

The alternative to exact numerical CME solvers is moment closure. Researchers like Lakatos and Stumpf have developed extensive frameworks for multivariate moment closure techniques applied to stochastic kinetic models.28 These packages (e.g., the MEANS python package) generate a closed set of ordinary differential equations tracking the temporal evolution of the system's statistical moments.28  
A critical analytical distinction must be made regarding the nature of the moments being tracked. Moment closure algorithms in chemical kinetics propagate the *unconditional moments of the state distribution* (i.e., the expected concentration of species $A$ at time $t$ across the entire ensemble).28 They inherently rely on a macroscopic approximation (e.g., assuming a Gaussian or log-normal distribution to close the system of higher-order moment equations). In stark contrast, the target algorithm computes the *exact conditional moments of a path-integral observable*, conditioned strictly upon precise multinomial start and end states over an interval $\\Delta t$, without making any distributional assumptions.

## **4\. Tensor Decompositions for CME / CTMC Ensembles**

To alleviate the curse of dimensionality inherent in the $M$-dimensional symmetric subspace, numerical linear algebra relies heavily on tensor network formalisms. Tensor Train (TT), Quantized Tensor Train (QTT), and Hierarchical Tucker (HT) decomposition methods are explicitly engineered to handle state spaces where operators factorize as Kronecker products.  
In tensor format literature, a system of $N$ non-interacting entities possesses a total generator expressed as $Q\_{\\text{total}} \= Q \\oplus \\dots \\oplus Q$, which dictates that the corresponding transition operator rigorously satisfies the identity $\\exp(Q\_{\\text{total}} \\Delta t) \= P(\\Delta t) \\otimes \\dots \\otimes P(\\Delta t)$. Tensor train methods compress this massive $S^N$ labeled state space by expressing the resulting probability vector as a contracted network of lower-rank parameter tensors, relying on repeated Singular Value Decompositions to truncate the entanglement between subsets of variables.  
While these tensor networks operate on an identical foundational algebraic principle—Kronecker factorization of non-interacting systems—their application in the stochastic process literature diverges fundamentally from the proposed target algorithm. Tensor methods are generally deployed exclusively to solve the forward Kolmogorov equations to obtain the unconditional, marginal probability distribution vector at time $t$. They do not compute, track, or propagate the boundary-conditioned conditional moments (mean and variance) of an integrated observable. Furthermore, standard tensor formats (TT, HT) operate by compressing the full labeled tensor product space $S^N$. They do not natively execute exact analytical projections onto the dimensionally minimized exact symmetric subspace $M$. Consequently, no documented tensor network algorithm extracts the required specific differentiable boundary-conditioned moment bundle necessary for exact HMM likelihood inference.

## **5\. Identical-Particle Propagators in Physics and Quantum Many-Body Systems**

The geometric projection of $N$ independent, indistinguishable particles into a multinomial state space is universally axiomatic in quantum mechanics, specifically formalized as second quantization. For an ensemble of non-interacting bosons, the multiparticle Hilbert space is exactly the symmetric subspace $\\text{Sym}^N(\\mathcal{H})$ of the tensor product space, and the natural basis is the occupation-number basis on the Fock space.

### **5.1 Recursive Projections and Symmetric Subspaces**

The abstract principle that a many-body propagator of a non-interacting system is derived purely from the symmetric projection of tensor products of single-particle propagators is canonical physics.30 Furthermore, recursive tensor composition algorithms are the bedrock of quantum condensed matter simulation. The Numerical Renormalization Group (NRG), originally developed by Kenneth Wilson, recursively diagonalizes Hamiltonians by forming tensor products of energy modes and projecting them onto dimensionally constrained subspaces at each iterative step.31 Similar recursive binary-tree subspace projections are utilized in Matrix Product States (MPS) and the generation of Affleck-Kennedy-Lieb-Tasaki (AKLT) states.31  
More recently, the era of quantum machine learning has introduced differentiable programming into symmetric subspace physics. Differentiable learning architectures for quantum circuit Born machines and the optimization of Koopman invariant subspaces explicitly parameterize symmetric subspace decompositions and propagate gradients through these specific projection operators.33

### **5.2 Translation to Classical Stochastic Processes**

Despite the profound mathematical equivalence of the underlying geometric spaces, translating these exact recursive subspace projection techniques from quantum mechanics to the classical stochastic CTMC literature for the purpose of HMM inference represents a massive and highly non-trivial gap.  
In quantum mechanics, the fundamental objects undergoing recursive symmetric composition are probability amplitudes (wavefunctions) or density matrices.30 The proposed target algorithm recursively composes a tightly coupled classical bundle consisting of transition probabilities, conditional expectations, and conditional variances of a real-valued integrated stochastic path. The critical analytical lemma underpinning the proposed algorithm—demonstrating that the centered second moment (conditional variance) remains strictly additive without cross-terms across the multinomial convolution due to conditional independence given the specific partitioned boundary state—is a purely probabilistic insight.39 It does not possess a direct physical analogue in the recursive projection of quantum wave mechanics.

## **6\. Filtering and Bayesian Inference for Compositional / Multinomial-State HMMs**

Within the domains of statistical ecology, epidemiology, and population dynamics, continuous-time Markov chains tracking populations universally exhibit multinomial state spaces (e.g., $N$ total individuals strictly compartmentalized into Susceptible, Infected, or Recovered states, yielding a conserved population). Bayesian inference within these models is fundamentally a filtering problem for Hidden Markov Models (HMMs).40  
Standard textbook treatments of HMMs (e.g., Cappé, Moulines, Rydén) rely on standard forward-backward algorithms to evaluate sequence likelihoods.22 When the state space is multinomial and $N$ is moderately large, the computational burden of propagating the forward probabilities scales identically to the ion channel problem. Consequently, the vast majority of ecological inference relies heavily on approximations (particle filtering with continuous noise approximations).42 When exact discrete inference is attempted, it necessitates building the full transition matrix via standard exponentiation.  
An exhaustive search of the literature involving compositional HMMs and Bayesian inference for population processes does not yield algorithms that explicitly factor the exact CTMC propagator across non-interacting subpopulations using a binary-doubling recursive logic to assemble the macroscopic likelihood.39 While some contemporary literature addresses the "recursive composition" of independent Markov chains to calculate specific likelihoods, it typically focuses on recursive formulations of optimal value functions in reinforcement learning (using Gaussian Processes) or branching processes counting reproductive lineages, rather than generating exact symmetric convolutions of identical, parallel, continuous-time state spaces.39

## **7\. Differentiable Matrix Exponential and Alternatives**

The core computational constraint that the target algorithm seeks to overcome is the notorious numerical instability associated with differentiating the matrix exponential. When computing $\\frac{\\partial}{\\partial \\theta} \\exp(Q\_{\\text{micro}} \\Delta t)$ using spectral theorems, the operation requires the diagonalization of $Q\_{\\text{micro}}$. If the generator possesses degenerate eigenvalues or a high condition number within its eigenvector matrix, the spectral derivative becomes numerically catastrophic. This is an omnipresent challenge in stiff kinetic systems across chemical master equations and ion channel patch-clamp experiments.

### **7.1 Modern Deep-Learning Stochastic Simulation Methods**

The necessity for achieving numerically stable, differentiable parameter inference in CTMC ensembles has catalyzed a massive wave of recent research at the intersection of machine learning and systems biology.4 The most significant, structurally adjacent, and contemporary breakthrough in this exact sub-domain is the mathematical framework published by Vilar and Saiz in February 2026, titled "Exact Discrete Stochastic Simulation with Deep-Learning-Scale Gradient Optimization".47  
Vilar and Saiz addressed the exact same fundamental limitation: the hard, categorical, discrete nature of stochastic event selection in Gillespie-type algorithms creates non-differentiable operations that strictly block standard backpropagation and gradient-based learning.47 To achieve massively parallel, autodiff-compatible CTMC inference without resorting to macroscopic mean-field approximations, Vilar and Saiz decoupled the forward simulation pass from the backward differentiation pass.49 They utilized a discrete Gumbel-Max reparameterization trick to generate exact forward trajectories of the CTMC, ensuring the capture of true intrinsic noise. On the backward pass, gradients are propagated through a continuous, heavily-parameterized Gumbel-Softmax straight-through surrogate.47 This relaxation mechanism allows the optimizer to smoothly "see through" the discrete state transitions to guide the kinetic parameters while keeping the forward simulation probabilistically exact.49 Their framework scaled successfully to train a 200,000-parameter gene regulatory network and was explicitly applied to fit experimental patch-clamp recordings of ion channel gating.49

### **7.2 The Distinction in Methodology**

While the underlying motivation of the Vilar and Saiz (2026) framework perfectly aligns with the motivation of the proposed target algorithm—enabling gradient-based, autodiff-compatible exact inference for highly discrete CTMC systems—the underlying methodology is diametrically opposed. Vilar and Saiz heavily rely on *stochastic simulation* (Monte Carlo sampling) paired with continuous reparameterization tricks (Gumbel-Softmax relaxations) to statistically estimate the gradient of the objective function.47  
Conversely, the proposed binary-doubling algorithm is an *exact, deterministic, numerical integration* method. It computes the absolute, closed-form analytical likelihood along with the exact boundary-conditioned moments of the entire discrete probability landscape. It achieves this strictly through the pure algebra of multinomial tensor convolutions. Because the macroscopic target observables are constructed systematically from standard additions and multiplications of the lowest-level microscopic variables ($P(\\Delta t), \\mu, \\sigma^2$), forward-mode automatic differentiation over the resulting computational graph is rendered trivial and perfectly stable by definition. The proposed algorithm completely avoids both the Monte Carlo sampling variance inherent in the Vilar and Saiz architecture and the defective spectral limits inherent in matrix eigendecompositions.

## **8\. Specific Analytical Questions Addressed**

This rigorous evaluation of the literature definitively addresses the core inquiries posed regarding algorithmic novelty:

1. **Has anyone published a binary-doubling-on-N (or equivalent recursive composition) construction for the boundary-conditioned moment bundle (transition probability \+ conditional mean \+ conditional variance of an integrated observable) of a non-interacting CTMC ensemble?** The precise construction does not exist anywhere in the published literature. While the high-level conceptual framework of recursive tensor composition and subsequent projection onto lower-dimensional symmetric subspaces is an established bedrock in quantum many-body physics (e.g., the Numerical Renormalization Group) 31, the technique has never been ported to classical stochastic processes for the specific intent of computing boundary-conditioned moments of integrated path observables. In domains that explicitly require these quantities—such as the ion channel inference literature led by Colquhoun, Hawkes, and Milescu—the mathematically identical identical system is modeled either via lossy macroscopic Gaussian approximations or by constructing the monolithic $M \\times M$ generator matrix.6  
2. **Has anyone explicitly noted the variance-additivity-under-conditional-independence-given-boundary-state observation, and used it to motivate tracking the centered second moment (rather than the raw second moment) through such a construction?** No. Standard macroscopic moment closure techniques (such as those developed by Lakatos and Stumpf) inherently track unconditional ensemble variances across populations.28 Single-channel burst-theory algorithms calculate total expected open-time variance, but they do not execute this computation via additive multinomial splits of a macroscopic ensemble. The explicit mathematical deduction that the *conditional* variance adds strictly linearly upon multinomial convolution—and its specific algorithmic utilization as a vital mechanism to prevent the exponential scaling of cross-terms during recursive propagator construction—is a genuine, unrecorded analytical insight completely unique to this algorithm.  
3. **Has anyone used independence-factorized symmetric-subspace propagators specifically for differentiable inference (gradient-based MLE, Fisher information, score, HMC), as opposed to forward simulation or non-differentiable likelihood evaluation?** No. While there is a massive contemporary push toward making discrete stochastic systems differentiable (perfectly exemplified by the February 2026 publications of Vilar and Saiz utilizing continuous Gumbel-Softmax relaxations 47), these approaches universally rely on stochastic sampling approximations of gradients. Deterministic exact inference relies fundamentally on the Finite State Projection or exact matrix exponentiation 23, both of which immediately encounter the severe differentiation instability of ill-conditioned and degenerate generators. The proposition to utilize pure symmetric recursive composition explicitly to create a differentiable, eigenvalue-free deterministic evaluation graph represents a highly novel algorithmic pathway.  
4. **Is there a closer field missing where this construction is standard practice?** No closer field is missing from this analysis. The closest applied domain is unquestionably the ion channel patch-clamp literature, specifically the "microscopic-recursive" likelihood framework originally formalized by Milescu (2005).12 This framework establishes the precise analytical target: computing the exact discrete likelihood for $N$ identical channels without succumbing to continuous approximations. The closest purely mathematical domain is quantum many-body physics, which has long utilized recursive composition for symmetrical tensor networks.31 The target algorithm acts as the undiscovered functional bridge connecting the pure geometry of quantum recursive symmetric subspaces to the applied biophysics of classical HMM exact inference.

## **9\. Synthesis and Final Conclusions**

The published work that comes closest is the microscopic-recursive likelihood framework of Milescu (2005) alongside the stochastic gradient optimization framework of Vilar and Saiz (2026), because the former formulates the requirement for exact discrete analytical inference over the $M$-dimensional symmetric subspace of $N$ identical CTMCs to bypass macroscopic approximation limits, while the latter targets the critical necessity of rendering these exact CTMC evaluations compatible with modern deep-learning automatic differentiation. The genuine gap is the absence of any deterministic, analytically closed-form algorithm capable of computing the specific boundary-conditioned path-integral moments exactly for $N \> 1$ that intrinsically avoids both stochastic gradient approximation and the numerical instability of $M$-dimensional matrix eigendecomposition. The strongest novelty claim defensible from this search is the introduction of the first exact, deterministic, recursive binary-doubling construction of the symmetric-subspace conditional moment bundle; an algorithm that uniquely imports symmetric subspace tensor geometry into classical HMM filtering, intrinsically bypasses the $M$-dimensional matrix exponential entirely, and enables absolutely numerically stable, forward-mode automatic differentiation for exact microscopic inference in macroscopic population CTMCs.
