# Kienker 1989 — Equivalence of aggregated Markov models of ion-channel gating

**Citation:** Kienker, P. (1989). Equivalence of aggregated Markov models of ion-channel gating. *Proceedings of the Royal Society of London. Series B, Biological Sciences*, 236(1284), 269–309. DOI: 10.1098/rspb.1989.0024.

**Verification status:** verified = true. Citation and every core claim below were confirmed against real sources (see Sources). Kienker's own PDF was paywalled (Royal Society 403, JSTOR 403), so all Kienker-attributed statements are taken from two secondary sources that reproduce his result precisely: Siekmann (2025), arXiv:2510.26728, whose text I converted and read directly, and Bruno, Yang & Pearson (2005, PNAS), fetched from PubMed Central. I did not read Kienker's abstract, lemma statements, or page-level wording first-hand.

## Core result

This is the canonical statement of source (B) non-identifiability: the map from rate matrix to observable is many-to-one. For an aggregated (lumped) continuous-time Markov model with the states partitioned into conductance classes, here closed C and open O, write the generator in block form

Q = [[Q_CC, Q_CO], [Q_OC, Q_OO]].

Kienker's equivalence relation is generated by **block-diagonal similarity transformations** that act within each conductance class:

S = diag(S_CC, S_OO),  with S_CC ∈ ℝ^{n_C×n_C}, S_OO ∈ ℝ^{n_O×n_O} invertible, off-diagonal blocks zero.

Two models are declared **equivalent** when Q′ = S⁻¹ Q S for such an S. Similarity guarantees the eigenvalues of Q (and of the diagonal blocks Q_CC, Q_OO) are invariant, so the relaxation-rate spectrum is shared. Kienker's Lemmata 1 and 2 go further: the whole family of stationary dwell-time distributions that define the observable behaviour, the open-time density f_O, closed-time density f_C, and the joint adjacent-interval densities f_OC and f_CO, are all preserved. Because those densities are the complete observable summary of the aggregated process (Fredkin, Montal & Rice 1985; Fredkin & Rice 1986), the two generators are experimentally indistinguishable from stationary single-channel records.

Structure and dimension of the equivalence class:
- The transformation group is the block-diagonal invertible group, a continuous group of dimension n_C² + n_O² (before quotienting by the stabilizer that fixes a given Q). So an equivalence class is a continuous **manifold** of rate matrices, not a finite list, though it also links distinct discrete topologies (the C-C-O and C-O-C three-state chains are equivalent).
- The observable behaviour depends on **at most 2·n_O·n_C** independent parameters (the identifiable dimension). A fully connected model on n_V = n_O + n_C states has (n_V)(n_V − 1) rate constants, so the gap (n_V)(n_V − 1) − 2·n_O·n_C generically measures the dimension of the unidentifiable fibre. Kienker's worked example, the fully connected three-state model (n_C = 2 closed, n_O = 1 open), has 6 rate constants but only 4 = 2·n_O·n_C identifiable, leaving a 2-parameter family of generators with identical single-channel statistics.
- **Canonical form (Bauer–Kienker uncoupled, BKU):** because Q_CC and Q_OO can be diagonalised (true under detailed balance), one can reparametrise any model to the representative in which all within-class transitions vanish, i.e. every transition runs between an open and a closed state, with exactly 2·n_O·n_C rates. This BKU form is a parameter-identifiable representative for each equivalence class. A refinement, the manifest-interconductance-rank (MIR) form of Bruno et al. 2005, uses the rank p_OC of Q_CO / Q_OC to give the sharper Fredkin–Rice count 2·p_OC·(n_O + n_C − p_OC).

On "metastate" / transformation-group language: the transformation-group statement is exactly the block-diagonal similarity group above. The term "metastate" is not Kienker's and does not appear in the sources read; the nearest concept is the abstract two-stage interpretation of the dwell-time densities (a finite mixture that selects among fast/slow exponential components), which is a reading of f_O, f_C, f_OC, f_CO, not a named object in Kienker.

## What is / is not identifiable

Identifiable from stationary single-channel data: the eigenvalue spectrum (relaxation rates) of Q, the numbers of resolvable open and closed exponential components (the model order), and up to 2·n_O·n_C combined rate parameters, i.e. the BKU/MIR canonical representative of the class.

Not identifiable: which specific generator inside the equivalence class produced the data. Anything that a conductance-class-preserving similarity change of basis can alter is invisible. This includes the connectivity/topology among states of the same conductance (C-C-O vs C-O-C), and, in general, the individual microscopic rate constants of a fully connected mechanism. A biophysically meaningful, labelled kinetic scheme is therefore under-determined by stationary dwell-time data beyond its canonical representative.

Feasibility caveat on the class: the reparametrisation to canonical form does not always land in the physically admissible set. For some data the BKU (and MIR) representative requires negative rates, so the class of physically realisable equivalent models can be strictly smaller than the algebraic similarity orbit.

## Relevance to the theory-model-observable picture (A mechanistic vs B aggregation)

This paper is the sharp, foundational statement of **source (B), the rate-matrix → observable degeneracy**. Distinct generators Q and Q′ = S⁻¹ Q S produce identical aggregated dwell-time statistics purely because the current only reports conductance class, not microstate. It quantifies (B): the identifiable content collapses from (n_V)(n_V − 1) rates to 2·n_O·n_C, and the residual is a continuous manifold plus discrete topology swaps.

It bears on **source (A), theory ↔ rate matrix**, only indirectly. Kienker does not model the theory-to-matrix step, but the canonical-form move exposes the same wound from the other side: the BKU representative is chosen for identifiability, not for mechanism, so passing to it discards the state labels and connectivity that carry biophysical meaning. Selecting a canonical form trades away exactly the mechanistic interpretation that (A) is about. So (A) and (B) compound: (B) says the data fix only an equivalence class of matrices, and (A) says each matrix already stood for many molecular stories.

Macroscopic vs single-channel observation, the axis that matters for the MacroIR program. Kienker's equivalence is defined on **stationary single-channel** observables (steady-state dwell-time distributions); the sources phrase it as models that "cannot be distinguished by steady-state experiments." Two consequences for a program that observes many-channel, interval-averaged currents:
1. The block-diagonal similarity transforms preserve the eigenvalue spectrum, hence the macroscopic relaxation time constants of the ensemble mean current are also shared. The purely spectral part of the Kienker degeneracy is not broken by going macroscopic.
2. Whether the extra macroscopic statistics (ensemble covariance / current-noise structure, and transient amplitudes under controlled initial conditions and agonist steps) shrink the class is not settled by Kienker and lies outside his stated scope. A similarity transform preserves the spectrum but can move eigenvector amplitudes; macroscopic non-stationary relaxation from a defined initial distribution weights those amplitudes, so it is plausible that macroscopic protocols resolve directions that stationary dwell-time data cannot. I flag this as an inference, not a Kienker result. This is precisely the frontier the MacroIR likelihood probes, and it is consistent with the authors' P2X2 experience, where a left-right coupling ambiguity remained macroscopically unidentifiable (a bimodal posterior) and had to be broken by importing an independent modality (molecular dynamics), an instance where macroscopic observation did not, on its own, collapse a (B)-type equivalence.

## Verbatim

From Siekmann (2025), arXiv:2510.26728, Section 3.2 (read directly from the converted PDF text):

"…even the probability distributions f_C, f_O, f_CO and f_OC are preserved under block-wise similarity transformations. This means that the dynamics D of a model Q′ = S⁻¹QS is indistinguishable from the model Q. For this reason, the models Q′ and Q are called equivalent."

"The matrix (37) defines a model structure where all transition rates from open to other open states or between closed states vanish—all transitions occur between open and closed states. This structure has been proposed as a canonical from [sic] for models with a given number of n_O open and n_C closed states. Canonical forms provide models that are parameter-identifiable and can be used as representatives for equivalence classes of models."

From Siekmann (2025), Section 4 (Discussion): canonical forms "might only be able to model some data sets if some rates are allowed to be negative."

From Bruno, Yang & Pearson (2005), PNAS (PMC1088360), reported via WebFetch of the article page: "the two topologies C-C-O and C-O-C are 'equivalent': for every set of rates for one there is a set of rates for the other such that both have identical steady-state behavior." The BKU form is described as one in which "every open state is connected to every closed state and vice versa, but there are no open-to-open or closed-to-closed connections."

## Caveats

- Kienker's primary text was not read first-hand (paywalled). Lemma numbers ("Lemmata 1 and 2"), the exact equivalence-preservation statement, and the 2·n_O·n_C bound are as reproduced by Siekmann (2025); the C-C-O/C-O-C phrasing and BKU definition are as reproduced by Bruno et al. (2005). Both are careful, well-cited sources, but for a manuscript-grade quotation of Kienker himself the RSPB PDF should be consulted.
- The tight upper bound on identifiable parameters is due to Fredkin & Rice (1986) (rank-refined), with Kienker's contribution being the explicit similarity-transform construction and the uncoupled canonical representative; do not attribute the sharp rank bound to Kienker alone.
- The macroscopic-vs-single-channel analysis in the "Relevance" section mixes a sourced fact (spectrum invariance under similarity) with an explicit inference about ensemble-covariance/transient information; the inference is mine, not Kienker's, and should be presented as an open question in any writeup.
- "Metastate" is not Kienker's term and appears in none of the sources read; avoid attributing it to this paper.
