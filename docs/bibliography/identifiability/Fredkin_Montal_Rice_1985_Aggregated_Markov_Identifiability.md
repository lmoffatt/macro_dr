# Fredkin, Montal & Rice (1985): Identification of aggregated Markovian models

**Citation.** Fredkin, D. R., Montal, M., & Rice, J. A. (1985). Identification of aggregated Markovian models: application to the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor. In L. M. Le Cam & R. A. Olshen (Eds.), *Proceedings of the Berkeley Conference in Honor of Jerzy Neyman and Jack Kiefer*, Vol. I (pp. 269-289). Belmont, CA: Wadsworth. The closely related theorem is restated and extended in Fredkin & Rice (1986), *Journal of Applied Probability* 23(1), 208-214.

## Core result

Notation. Let the channel have `n_O` open (conducting) states and `n_C` closed (non-conducting) states, total `N = n_O + n_C`. Let `Q` be the `N x N` continuous-time Markov generator (rate matrix) with off-diagonal entries `q_ij` the individual rate constants. The experiment observes only the aggregated two-level signal (open vs closed), so the raw record is the sequence of sojourn (dwell) times in the open class alternating with sojourn times in the closed class. Write `f_OC(s,t)` and `f_CO(s,t)` for the bivariate (joint) densities of an open interval of length `s` adjacent to a closed interval of length `t`, and vice versa.

Fredkin, Montal & Rice established the identifiability ceiling for this observation. Two statements, in increasing sharpness:

1. **Sufficiency of the adjacent-interval densities.** All the information the aggregated (open/closed) record carries about the dynamics, at steady state, is contained in the pair of bivariate densities `f_OC` and `f_CO`. Higher-order joint distributions of longer runs of intervals add nothing beyond what `f_OC`, `f_CO` already fix (under mild technical conditions that hold generically).

2. **Parameter-count ceiling: 2·n_O·n_C.** Those densities have the form of sums of products of exponentials. `f_OC(s,t) = Σ_{i,j} α_OC^{ij} exp(λ_O^i s) exp(λ_C^j t)`, and symmetrically for `f_CO`. The recoverable quantities are the open-class decay rates `λ_O^i` (i = 1..n_O), the closed-class decay rates `λ_C^j` (j = 1..n_C), and the amplitude matrices `α^{ij}`. Counting the independent numbers these can encode gives an upper bound of `2·n_O·n_C` on the number of parameters the steady-state aggregated data can determine. Equivalently, there are at most `2·n_O·n_C` degrees of freedom in the steady-state data.

The teeth of the result are in the comparison to how many parameters `Q` has. A fully connected generator on `N` states has `N(N-1) = (n_O + n_C)(n_O + n_C - 1)` free off-diagonal rates. This grows like `N²`, while the identifiable count `2·n_O·n_C` grows more slowly and is maximized (for fixed `N`) only in the balanced case. So for models beyond a small size, the rate matrix carries strictly more parameters than the data can fix, and any model exceeding `2·n_O·n_C` rate constants can be reparametrised without changing a single observable statistic. Worked case: the fully connected three-state model (`n_O = 1`, `n_C = 2`) has `N(N-1) = 6` rates but only `2·n_O·n_C = 4` identifiable, leaving a two-parameter family of observationally identical rate matrices. Siekmann (2025) treats exactly this example.

A later refinement (Bruno, Yang & Pearson 2005) sharpens the ceiling to `2R(n_O + n_C - R)`, where `R` is the "interconductance rank" (the rank of the block of `Q` coupling open to closed states). When the open-closed coupling is full rank this reduces toward the classical `2·n_O·n_C` scale; when the classes are connected through a low-rank bottleneck, far fewer parameters are identifiable.

## What is / is not identifiable

**Identifiable (structural, from single-channel steady-state dwell times):** the eigenvalue spectra `λ_O^i` and `λ_C^j` (the observable relaxation/decay rates within each conductance class), and the amplitude matrices `α^{ij}` of the adjacent-interval densities. These are `2·n_O·n_C` numbers at most. They constitute the equivalence-class label: two generators that agree on them are observationally indistinguishable.

**Not identifiable:** the individual rate constants `q_ij`, and in general the topology (graph) of `Q`. Fredkin, Montal & Rice show the map from `Q` to the observable law is many-to-one. The pre-image is an equivalence class generated by block-wise similarity transformations that preserve `f_OC` and `f_CO` while moving the entries and even the connectivity of `Q`. Siekmann (2025) separates two questions inside this: (i) *parameter* identifiability, can the `q_ij` be recovered uniquely for a fixed graph, and (ii) *structural* identifiability, can `Q` be reparametrised onto a genuinely different graph `G'` that produces the same dynamics. Even when the answer to (i) is "unique up to a finite set," the equivalence class can still contain several distinct models, so the observable does not pick out one mechanism.

**Structural identifiability vs parameter estimation.** The `2·n_O·n_C` ceiling is a statement of *structural* (a priori, model-and-observable) identifiability: it is a property of the exact, noise-free, infinite-data law, independent of any estimator or finite sample. It says the parameters are not a function of the observable distribution at all, so no amount of data and no estimator can separate members of the equivalence class. This is upstream of, and categorically different from, parameter *estimation*, which concerns the variance/bias of recovering an identifiable parameter from a finite, noisy record. A model can be structurally identifiable yet practically unestimable (flat likelihood, huge error bars); Fredkin-Montal-Rice is about the prior case, where the likelihood is not merely flat but exactly invariant along the equivalence class.

## Relevance to the theory-model-observable picture (A mechanistic vs B aggregation)

This paper is *the* canonical statement of source (B), aggregation non-identifiability: distinct rate matrices `Q` produce identical aggregated observable statistics. Its entire content lives at the rate-matrix-to-observable arrow. Within its framework a "model" is a generator `Q` plus a labelling of states as open or closed; it deliberately attaches no molecular meaning to states.

It bounds (B) two ways at once: it tells you the size of the identifiable summary (`2·n_O·n_C` numbers) and it exhibits the equivalence class (the similarity-transform orbit) that the data cannot penetrate. Its "structural identifiability" sub-question, whether `Q` can be moved to a non-equivalent graph `G'`, reaches one layer up toward mechanism, because a graph/topology of conformational states is closer to a molecular story than a bare rate matrix is. But it does not address source (A) proper, the theory-to-rate-matrix arrow: it says nothing about different molecular narratives that map onto the same `Q`, nor about which physical state a mathematical state denotes. (A) and (B) compound; this paper quantifies only (B) and the topology sliver adjacent to it.

**Single-channel vs macroscopic observation.** Read the scope carefully: the `2·n_O·n_C` theorem is specifically for *single-channel* dwell-time (sojourn-time) data *at equilibrium/steady state*. Siekmann (2025) is explicit that the bound "is only valid for processes that are at equilibrium," and argues that single-channel steady-state data alone are a weak basis for pinning a biophysical mechanism; non-stationary protocols (rapid ligand-concentration jumps) and modal-gating analysis inject information the steady-state dwell-time law cannot.

The MacroIR program observes a different functional of `Q`: the interval-averaged current of many channels, i.e. the ensemble mean plus fluctuation (second-moment) statistics, generally under non-stationary agonist protocols. This changes the identifiability accounting rather than inheriting the `2·n_O·n_C` number directly, and the direction of change cuts both ways. The macroscopic *mean* relaxation exposes the same class eigenvalues `λ` but only through their projection onto the single conductance vector, a rank-limited readout that resolves the amplitude matrices `α^{ij}` less finely than adjacent single-channel intervals do; on that axis the mean macroscopic signal is weaker than single-channel dwell times. Working the other way, many-channel fluctuation analysis restores second moments, and non-stationary jump protocols break the equilibrium premise of the theorem, both adding identifiable directions. The upshot for the program's caveat is that the Fredkin-Montal-Rice equivalence class is not automatically the MacroIR equivalence class, but the qualitative lesson transfers: the observable returns an equivalence class of rate matrices, never a unique mechanism, and the residual ambiguity has to be closed by importing an independent modality. The P2X2 left-right coupling ambiguity resolved by molecular dynamics (Moffatt & Pierdominici-Sottile, Comm Biol 2025) is a concrete macroscopic instance of exactly this pattern. Note that the specific macroscopic accounting in this paragraph is my analysis by extension; Fredkin, Montal & Rice prove only the single-channel steady-state case.

## Verbatim

From Bruno, W. J., Yang, J., & Pearson, J. E. (2005), *PNAS* 102(18):6326-6331 (secondary source citing Fredkin, Montal & Rice):
- "It has long been known that there are at most 2N_oN_c degrees of freedom in steady-state data."
- Fredkin et al. "showed that f_oc and f_co are sufficient to fully characterize the steady-state data, under certain technical conditions that are almost always satisfied."

From Siekmann, I. (2025), arXiv:2510.26728, Section 3.1 (secondary source re-deriving and attributing the result to Fredkin et al. 1985 and Fredkin & Rice 1986):
- "all information on the dynamics of an aggregated Markov model is contained in the bivariate distributions f_OC and f_CO"
- "in total there are at most 2 n_O n_C independent parameters in the distributions f_OC and f_CO" (their eq. 32)
- Abstract: "models with n_O open and n_C closed states that exceed the maximum number 2 n_O n_C of parameters are non-identifiable"

## Caveats

- Content is sourced from secondary literature, not the 1985 primary. Treat the `2·n_O·n_C` ceiling, the sufficiency of `f_OC`/`f_CO`, and the equivalence-class framing as well corroborated (two independent authoritative sources, one with equations); treat any exact wording or page/theorem locator of the 1985 paper as unverified.
- Page range is reported as 269-289 by some sources and 269-290 by others.
- The bound is a *steady-state, single-channel* result. Do not transplant the literal number `2·n_O·n_C` onto macroscopic, non-stationary, or fluctuation-analysed observations; the identifiable set there must be recomputed. The refined `2R(n_O+n_C-R)` bound (Bruno et al. 2005) shows the count is itself sensitive to model structure (the open-closed coupling rank `R`).
- The result addresses aggregation (B), not the theory-to-model arrow (A). Non-identifiability in the full program is at least the compounding of both.
- "Fredkin, Montal & Rice 1985" (Berkeley proceedings) and "Fredkin & Rice 1986" (J. Appl. Probab. 23:208-214) are distinct but companion works; the 1986 paper carries the sharper measure-theoretic statement of the same identifiability structure. Do not conflate either with the NIST J. Res. NBS 90(6):517 (1985) item that surfaced in searches, whose authorship/content I did not verify.
