# Milescu, Akk & Sachs (2005): Maximum Likelihood Estimation of Ion Channel Kinetics from Macroscopic Currents

**Citation:** Milescu, L. S., Akk, G., & Sachs, F. (2005). Maximum Likelihood Estimation of Ion Channel Kinetics from Macroscopic Currents. *Biophysical Journal*, 88(4), 2494–2515. doi:10.1529/biophysj.104.053256.

**Verification status:** Verified against the repository PDF, read directly (title page plus the identifiability-bearing Introduction, Results, and Discussion sections, pages 2494-2513). Citation metadata confirmed on the title page and by WebSearch (PMC1305347, ScienceDirect S0006349505733051). Quotes below are verbatim with page locators.

This is the authoritative reference for direct maximum-likelihood estimation of rate constants from macroscopic (many-channel, ensemble) currents, and it is by two of the co-authors (Sachs) in the lineage this codebase extends. It is the closest prior art to what MacroIR does at the observable level: fit rate constants to the mean and variance of ensemble current under driving stimuli, treating channel count and unitary current as estimable quantities. Its whole framing of identifiability is exactly source (B) below.

## Core result

The authors formulate the non-stationary macroscopic current I_t as a Gaussian random variable whose mean μ_t and variance V_t are functions of the rate matrix Q, the channel count N_C, the unitary current mean and variance, and the (arbitrary, possibly finite-rise-time) stimulation protocol, and they maximize the likelihood of the whole current trace directly for the rate constants (plus N_C and unitary-current parameters). The mean is μ_t = μ⁰ + N_C·(Pᵗ·μ) and the variance carries both a baseline/intrinsic term and a state-fluctuation term N_C·Vˣ (Eqs. 6-13; Fig. Conductance model, p.2496).

The central identifiability message: fitting the ensemble current under variable, non-equilibrium stimuli, and using the current's variance and not only its mean, sharply increases what can be identified relative to equilibrium data or mean-only fits. From the abstract (p.2494): "Utilizing arbitrary stimulation protocols and using the mean and the variance of the current reduce or eliminate problems of model identifiability (Kienker, 1989)." They demonstrate this on the two minimum non-trivial ligand-gated schemes, C-C-O (closed-closed-open) and C-O-C (closed-open-closed), across three stimulation protocols P1, P2, P3 of increasing complexity (Figs. 1-5).

## What is / is not identifiable

The paper splits identifiability into two questions (p.2500): (i) can two same-size, different-topology models be distinguished under a given protocol; (ii) does a fixed topology have a unique parameter+N_C solution, or multiple optima. It adopts the Cobelli-DiStefano (1980) classification: uniquely identifiable / non-uniquely identifiable / non-identifiable.

Identifiable from macroscopic currents:
- **The number of kinetic states N_S**, from the number of relaxation exponentials. Topology is *not* recoverable this way: "fitting with exponentials cannot be used to determine model topology. Only the number of kinetic states N_S can be inferred from the number of exponential components" (p.2510).
- **Topology, given sufficiently rich non-stationary stimuli.** Under equilibrium, C-C-O and C-O-C are indistinguishable (Kienker); under a driving step they differ (C-C-O has a second-order latency, C-O-C a first-order latency) and the authors could not find any C-C-O parameter set reproducing a C-O-C simulated trace, or vice versa (p.2500).
- **Rate constants + N_C + unitary current mean/variance**, but only up to a degree that depends on both the topology and the protocol. Their concrete tabulation (p.2501): C-C-O is non-identifiable with P1 and P2 and uniquely identifiable only with the three-step P3; C-O-C is non-identifiable with P1, non-uniquely identifiable with P2, uniquely identifiable with P3. "C-C-O is more ambiguous than C-O-C for the same stimulation."
- **N_C alone**, even from stationary macroscopic data, via the variance (non-stationary/stationary noise analysis in the Sigworth sense); the method "could be used as a simple calculator of N_C from stationary macroscopic data, without any knowledge of the model" if the single-channel amplitude is known (p.2513).

Not identifiable / residual macroscopic degeneracies:
- **N_C and the unitary amplitude μ are confounded in the mean.** The mean depends only on the product N_C·μ, so "N_C and μ cannot be estimated simultaneously from the average alone (i.e., with the SS method), and the single-channel noise vector V cannot be estimated at all" (p.2513). The variance breaks this: with mean+variance, "any quantity of the triplet N_C, μ, and V can be estimated, but how many of these can be estimated simultaneously depends on the identifiability of the model, and on the quality of the data" (p.2513).
- **Rate constants and N_C trade off** when N_C is inferred from peak amplitude; Fig. 3 shows explicitly that "macroscopic currents of identical mean (but not variance) can be obtained with different sets of the five parameters (four rate constants and N_C)" (Fig. 3 caption, p.2501).
- **Equilibrium-protocol degeneracy.** At zero-ligand equilibrium (P1) the resting occupancy is fixed by the absence of ligand and carries no information about the four rate constants; a finite-ligand equilibrium (P2) does depend on all four and is more informative (p.2512). This is why simpler protocols leave a continuum (P1) or a discrete set (P2) of solutions.

Two levers increase identifiability (p.2502, p.2511): add equations by (a) using the variance, not only the mean, and/or (b) using more complex stimulation protocols; or reduce free parameters by imposing physically motivated constraints (positivity, identical/independent binding sites in a fixed ratio, microscopic reversibility of cycles). Constraints made an otherwise non-identifiable C-C-C-O AChR model identifiable (p.2510). Because there is "no easy way to theoretically determine the parameter identifiability for a given model and a given stimulation protocol," they propose an empirical test: refit from many random starts, and separately perturb-and-fix one parameter, to detect whether a second solution of equal likelihood exists (p.2512).

## Relevance to the theory-model-observable picture (A mechanistic vs B aggregation)

**Source (B), aggregation (rate matrix -> observable): this paper is squarely about (B), for the macroscopic observable specifically.** Its identifiability discussion is entirely at the level of rate matrices and observable current statistics. The Kienker (1989) result it invokes is a (B)-type equivalence: "two models of different topology cannot be distinguished under equilibrium conditions if their Q matrices are related by a similarity transform" (p.2500), with C-C-O and C-O-C giving "identical likelihood" on equilibrium data. The key twist for this research program is *how macroscopic observation changes (B) relative to single-channel dwell times*:
- The Kienker degeneracy is an *equilibrium* statement. Driving the ensemble out of equilibrium with time-varying stimuli, and reading the full relaxation (mean) plus fluctuations (variance), breaks it. So macroscopic *non-stationary* fitting is strictly more discriminating than equilibrium single-channel dwell-time analysis of the same scheme. This is the empirical backbone of the abstract's "reduce or eliminate problems of model identifiability."
- But macroscopic observation introduces its own degeneracies that single-channel recording does not have, notably the N_C·μ amplitude confound (single-channel recording measures μ directly). The variance repairs it only partially and model-dependently.
- Their likelihood uses only the diagonal of the autocovariance and treats the trace as a function of the initial-state probabilities alone (no dwell-time information, hence no missed-event correction needed; p.2511). This makes the macroscopic likelihood cheaper but slightly biased (local-time correlation ignored, Figs. 8, 10, 12); the bias is an accuracy issue, distinct from identifiability.

**Source (A), mechanistic (theory <-> rate matrix): the paper does not address it.** It never asks which molecular story a state "means"; a state is a slot in Q with an on/off conductance label. It offers no statement that one rate matrix can correspond to several molecular theories, nor that an underspecified theory maps to several matrices. The nearest adjacency is its treatment of over-parameterized models: spurious extra states "are generally signaled by the optimizer by practically zero entry rates, or by very large exit rates" and can be pruned by AIC/BIC (pp.2508, 2512-2513). That is model-complexity control, not the theory-to-matrix many-to-one mapping. So for the program's caveat, this work supports and quantifies (B) for macroscopic currents but is silent on (A).

Bearing on the program: MacroIR observes interval-averaged (integrated) macroscopic currents, the regime Milescu et al. analyze. Their results say this regime is quite identifying when driven and when variance is used, yet genuine residual degeneracies remain (amplitude-vs-count; topology needing complex protocols; equilibrium ambiguities). The P2X2 left-right coupling ambiguity in Moffatt & Pierdominici-Sottile (Comm. Biol. 2025), which stayed bimodal under macroscopic fitting and needed molecular dynamics to resolve, is exactly a residual (B)-type degeneracy of the kind this paper shows is not automatically removed by going macroscopic + non-stationary + variance.

## Caveats

- The between-topology distinguishability of C-C-O vs C-O-C under non-stationary stimuli is shown empirically (they could not find degenerate parameter sets by search), not proven: "our search approach was not designed to find all admissible solutions rigorously" (p.2501). Treat it as strong evidence, not a theorem.
- The unique-identifiability tallies (two/three free parameters for C-C-O under P1/P2, etc.) are results of a numerical search over zero-SS solutions on noiseless simulations, and are specific to these 3-state schemes and these three protocols. They are illustrative, not general theorems about macroscopic identifiability.
- What the paper attributes to Kienker (1989) here is the equilibrium similarity-transform equivalence of whole rate matrices; that is not verbatim identical to the Fredkin/Colquhoun-Hawkes aggregated-dwell-time equivalence theory. Cite it as the paper states it.
- Macroscopic MLE estimates are shown to be biased (asymmetric, cross-correlated) because the method ignores the current's local time correlation (Figs. 8, 10, 12; p.2513). That is an estimation-accuracy limitation, separate from the identifiability content, but worth flagging when this paper is used to argue "macroscopic is enough."
- The paper is silent on mechanistic (A) non-identifiability (theory <-> rate matrix). Do not cite it for (A).
