# Title — MacroIR validity paper (eLife 2025)

## CHOSEN (for now)

> ## Information distortion in likelihood approximations for macroscopic ion-channel currents

**"for", not "of".** What is approximated is the *likelihood*, not the currents. "Approximations **of** macroscopic currents" invites the misreading that the currents are being approximated. "for" says the approximations serve that data type, with no ambiguity.

**Hyphen note.** "ion-channel currents" hyphenates the compound modifier, which is grammatically correct. Your own papers write it open ("ion channel kinetics", Moffatt 2007). Either is fine; pick one and stay consistent with the rest of the manuscript.

### Why this one

- **It is in the register of the field, and of your own papers.** The canon is plain, functional noun phrases, no aphorism and no colon drama: "On the stochastic properties of single ion channels" (Colquhoun & Hawkes), "Maximum likelihood estimation of ion channel kinetics from macroscopic currents" (Milescu), "Bayesian inference of kinetic schemes for ion channels by Kalman filtering" (Münch), "Estimation of ion channel kinetics from fluctuations of macroscopic currents" (Moffatt 2007), "Bayesian inference of functional asymmetry in the homotrimeric ligand-gated ion channel P2X2" (Moffatt 2025). This title has the same shape as your 2007 paper. **(Partly corrected, see Evidence 1 below: this holds for the ion-channel era, but the honeybee papers (1997-2001) used a declarative finding and a colon+question. The bold register is your early voice, and it belongs to your most-cited work.)**
- **It names the novel object** (information distortion), which is the genuine contribution, and not the method (which was already published in Comm Biol).
- **It names the domain** in the last four words, so the ion-channel audience finds it.
- **It carries no AI tells**: no antithetical parallel, no colon subtitle, no coined compound doing rhetorical work.
- The **hook lives in the abstract's first line**, not the title. The title does not have to do all the work, and buying a hook at the price of sounding like a different genre is a bad trade.

### Runner-up, if you later want more edge without leaving the register

> How likelihood approximations distort kinetic information in time-averaged ion-channel currents

Same register, slightly more pointed (a declarative "distort"), still domain-forward.

## Rules that came out of the review (they also govern the abstract)

Four AI reviews (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, DeepSeek) plus a nine-agent judge panel converged on these. They are accuracy constraints, not taste:

1. **Distortion, not loss.** The effect is bidirectional: NR inflates variance (over-counts information), NMR deflates, MR overestimates, R has a residual factor ~2, IR reaches ~1. Any phrasing built on "information loss" or "preserving information" is factually wrong.
2. **The approximation distorts, not time-averaging.** Time averaging is the physical reality that the recursive interval likelihood handles correctly. Blaming time-averaging (as in "when does time averaging distort information") inverts the causation and contradicts the thesis.
3. **Continuous, not a phase transition.** The distortion grows gradually toward the multinomial and telegraphic corners. Do not imply a sharp threshold ("fail", "breaks down at").
4. **No "only", no universal claims.** The study is two-state; "only X stays calibrated" invites a scope objection.
5. **No method promotion.** The paper characterizes; it does not re-present MacroIR. The bridge is already published.

## Rejected, and why

**"Markov models are instantaneous, ion-channel currents never are: an information-distortion map of the likelihoods that bridge them"**
Memorable and it carried Luciano's own framing, but: the antithetical parallel is a construction LLMs reach for constantly (and the style rules flag contrastive constructions); "the likelihoods that bridge them" is over-constructed; and above all it is out of register for electrophysiology, where titles are plain. It reads as science communication, not as a paper in this field.

**Sharpened by Evidence 2:** eLife itself uses **zero colons** in 20 surveyed titles, and zero aphorisms in any field. So the disqualifying feature is the **antithetical cadence**, not the boldness. Boldness is fully available through the declarative finding, which is both eLife's dominant shape and Luciano's own most-cited device.

**Worth preserving from it, though:** "the likelihoods that bridge them" is the most precise naming of the object of study produced in this whole review. It says *why* those likelihoods exist and *what for*, which "likelihood approximations" does not. And "map" is the right epistemic word: continuous, cartographic, a characterization rather than an announced solution. Any final title should try to keep that precision.

**"A bridge between instantaneous Markov models and unavoidable averaged experimental data"** (Luciano's original)
Great statement of the problem, but it sells the bridge instead of characterizing it, and the bridge is exactly what Comm Biol already published. A reviewer from the field bites the hook and says "you published this". Also a verbless noun phrase, which reads as a Perspective rather than a research article.

**ChatGPT's G4 and G5** ("Information loss defines the validity…", "When does time averaging distort statistical information…") fail rules 1 and 2 outright and were discarded by the panel's specialist judge.

**The pattern, in 20 titles:**
- **Zero colons.** Not one. Named tools take a **comma + apposition** ("Celldetective, an AI-enhanced image analysis tool for…"), never a colon.
- **Zero questions. Zero aphorisms. Zero antitheses.**
- **Dominant shape, over half: the declarative finding.** Subject + verb + object: X *reveals* Y, X *is central to* Y, X *shapes* Y, X *contributes to* Y, X *resolves* Y.
- Secondary: noun phrase ("Characterization of…", "A membrane insertion code for…").
- Tertiary: gerund ("Rescuing…", "Benchmarking…").

**The convergence.** eLife's dominant shape is the declarative finding, which is exactly the device of Luciano's most-cited paper ("Oxygen consumption … **depends on** the reward rate"). His most-cited voice and the journal's house style are the same grammar.

## Revised recommendation, given the evidence

The CHOSEN title above is a **noun phrase**, which is eLife's *secondary* shape (compare "A membrane insertion code for intrinsically disordered proteins"). It is in style and remains a perfectly valid choice.

But the evidence now favours the **declarative finding**, on three independent grounds: it is eLife's dominant shape, it is Luciano's most-cited voice, and it *states* the contribution rather than merely naming it.

> ## Likelihood approximations distort the kinetic information in macroscopic ion-channel currents

Subject, verb, object, domain. Eleven words. The same grammar as "Lipid packing contributes to the confinement of caveolae" and as the 1997 honeybee paper. No colon, no aphorism, no question. It says the finding, names the threat, points at the approximations, and does not sell the method.

If the distortion machinery is to be **named** as an object (as "the Repeater Stations Model" was in 2020), the eLife device is the comma-apposition, never the colon:

> The information-distortion map, a diagnostic for likelihood approximations of macroscopic ion-channel currents

## Option bank (kept as the record)

### A. Finding-forward, validity (no MacroIR)

**A1. The validity limits of macroscopic likelihood approximations for time-averaged stochastic currents**
- Pros: precise; states the contribution; the technical anchors are right.
- Cons: dry and slightly negative; no answer hinted.

**A2. Where Gaussian likelihood approximations for time-averaged currents succeed and fail**
- Pros: crisp succeed/fail thesis; accessible.
- Cons: "fail" implies a threshold (rule 3); gives away no resolution.

### B. Question-forward

**B1. How reliable are the likelihood approximations used for time-averaged ion-channel currents?**
- Pros: signals characterization; honest and inviting.
- Cons: a question can read as non-committal; no answer hinted.

### C. Information-distortion / diagnostic

**C1. Information distortion in time-averaged kinetic inference**
- Pros: names the novel object; short; portable across the program.
- Cons: no domain cue, so the channel audience does not see itself. (Fixed by the chosen title, which is C1 plus the domain.)

**C2. A Fisher-information diagnostic for time-averaged likelihood approximations**
- Pros: names the tool.
- Cons: tool-forward, dry, "Fisher information" is jargon in a title.

### D. MacroIR-forward

**D1. MacroIR and the limits of macroscopic likelihood approximations for time-averaged currents**
- Cons: splits focus; re-centers on the method (rule 5).

**D2. Only recursive interval likelihoods stay calibrated for time-averaged macroscopic currents**
- Cons: "Only" breaks rule 4; method promotion.

### E. Concept-forward

**E1. Two Gaussian approximations set when time-averaged kinetic inference can be trusted**
- Cons: needs unpacking; awkward English.

### F. Program-tied

**F1. From molecular mechanisms to data: validating the likelihood step of kinetic inference**
- Cons: over-promises; the program framing should stay light.
