\section*{Results}

The results follow one order, in which each figure answers the objection the previous one raises. The four recursive approximations differ visibly in a single filter step (Figure 1); that difference is miscalibrated parameter uncertainty, and a control isolates which part of the interval structure causes it (Figure 2); the miscalibration lives in the likelihood itself, interval by interval (Figure 3); it comes from correlation across time rather than any per-sample error, and it persists across the design space (Figure 4); and the calibrated member turns into advice an experimenter can act on (Figure 5).

\subsection*{A minimal two-state model and the recursive ladder}

We keep the model at the smallest size that still poses the estimation problem, so that any miscalibration we measure is a property of the likelihood approximation and not of the model. A patch of $N_{\mathrm{ch}}$ identical channels, each switching between a closed and an open state ($\mathrm{closed}\rightleftharpoons\mathrm{open}$) at an agonist-dependent opening rate $k_{\mathrm{on}}$ and a closing rate $k_{\mathrm{off}}$, is driven by a step change in agonist concentration; only the open state conducts. The recorded current is the patch current averaged over each measurement interval, with white instrumental noise added. That interval average is the object every approximation in this paper has to account for. Exact stochastic simulation of the channel population supplies the ground truth, because the forward problem of generating a trajectory is tractable where the inverse problem of writing down the exact likelihood of an averaged recording is not.

The four approximations are one recursive filter run under four settings of what the interval-averaged conductance is conditioned on, and they form a ladder. R (Recursive) uses the conductance at a single instant and ignores the averaging. MR (Mean Recursive) uses the interval-mean conductance conditioned on the state at the interval's start, with the total per-start-state conductance variance. VR (Variance Recursive) keeps MR's mean and its update but replaces that total variance with the residual one, the part left after conditioning on where the interval ends. IR (Interval Recursive) conditions on the state at both interval endpoints, which lets the covariance between them enter the update. The recursion is held fixed across all four, so each column adds one piece of interval structure to the one before it; the two non-recursive members of the family are named in the Theory section and measured in no figure of this paper. Figure 1 lays one turn of the filter cycle side by side, the prior, the predicted current, the Bayesian update and the accumulated log-likelihood; it makes the coordinate system concrete and carries no claim about accuracy. VR's predicted band is already visibly the narrowest wherever the channels gate, which is the mechanism the next figure measures.

\subsection*{Which conditioning is calibrated: parameter recovery}

Figure 2 turns the ladder into a statement about parameter uncertainty. From ten thousand independent recordings of a patch of $N_{\mathrm{ch}}=100$ channels, each of the four parameters (the rates $k_{\mathrm{on}}$ and $k_{\mathrm{off}}$, the unitary current $i$, and the channel number $N_{\mathrm{ch}}$) is re-estimated by maximum likelihood under each rung, and the spread of those estimates is compared with the uncertainty each rung reports from its own Fisher information. The two are different objects.
The ratio of the realized spread to the reported one, the overconfidence factor, is $1.32$ for R, $1.97$ for MR and $2.18$ for VR on the kinetic pair, against $1.02$ for IR; on the amplitude pair it is $1.09$, $1.53$ and $1.77$ against $1.00$. % src: Figure_2_caption.md
IR alone is calibrated: its reported and realized uncertainties coincide across every one of the four parameters, and over every pair its three ellipses (empirical, Fisher, sandwich-corrected) sit on top of one another with the estimates centred on the truth. % src: Figure_2_caption.md

The ordering is the point, and it is not monotone in the naive direction. VR is the most over-confident member of the ladder, worse than MR, even though it corrects MR's variance toward the interval-conditioned one. % src: Figure_2_caption.md
It removes the part of the predicted observable variance that conditioning on the interval endpoint would explain, without taking the boundary term in the gain that would justify removing it, so the reported uncertainty degrades rather than improves. The step that recovers calibration is therefore the gain, the boundary conditioning IR adds in the update, and not the variance form; VR is the control that isolates this, and it is why the roster includes it.

The distortion is measurable and also removable. Rescaling each reported covariance by the observed covariance of the score, the sandwich correction, returns every rung, IR included, to a spread ratio within a tenth of one. % src: Figure_2_caption.md
That one correction repairs the whole ladder is what makes the distortion a usable quantity rather than a complaint. A natural worry, that the clouds are driven by a handful of atypical recordings, does not hold: within each cloud the maximized log-likelihood of a recording is essentially uncorrelated with how far its estimate lands from the truth (Figure 2--figure supplement 1), because the log-likelihood measures how atypical a realization's noise and gating happened to be, not how well its parameters were recovered.

\subsection*{Time-resolved calibration and the score/Fisher checks}

The miscalibration is present in the likelihood itself, interval by interval, and is not an artifact of the fit. Scoring one common set of a thousand recordings interval by interval (Figure 3) shows first that the ensemble-mean total log-likelihood is $-143.4$ for IR, $-147.2$ for R, $-148.9$ for VR and $-153.9$ for MR, a span of $10.4$ nats with IR highest: under the fully conditioned filter the same data are about $e^{10}$ times more probable than under the mean-recursive one. % src: Figure_3_caption.md

The rest of the figure is the hinge of the argument. Per interval, every one of the four reports its information correctly: the variance of the per-interval score matches the per-interval Fisher information, $\mathrm{Var}(s_t)/F_t \approx 1$, for all of them. % src: Figure_3_caption.md
The failure appears only once the information is accumulated. The ratio of the variance of the summed score to the summed information reads $1.07$ (95\% CI $0.99$ to $1.17$) for R, $1.19$ ($1.10$ to $1.29$) for MR, $1.20$ ($1.09$ to $1.30$) for VR and $1.08$ ($1.00$ to $1.17$) for IR: MR and VR are the pair whose interval falls clear of one, and VR is no better than MR. % src: Figure_3_caption.md
So the local information content is right everywhere; the error is entirely in how the information adds up over time.

The reason is visible in the same figure. The per-interval score is correlated across time for every rung except IR: at lag one the score autocorrelation is $-0.004 \pm 0.004$ for IR, indistinguishable from zero, against $0.191 \pm 0.004$ for R, $0.256 \pm 0.004$ for VR and $0.357 \pm 0.004$ for MR. % src: Figure_3_caption.md
A correlated score makes the variance of its sum exceed the sum of the per-interval informations, which is why the per-interval identity can hold while the accumulated one fails. This temporal correlation of the current, which conditioning on both interval endpoints removes, is the empirical form of the diagnosis that Milescu and colleagues gave for the same failure, the local time correlation of the current \cite{milescu2005maximum}.
The data-level residual tells the same story: the mean squared standardized residual holds at one throughout for IR, sits below one for R and MR (which over-predict the spread), and holds at one for VR except for a sharp excursion at the concentration jump, where removing the between-endpoint variance costs most because the population is redistributing fastest. % src: Figure_3_caption.md

\subsection*{Where the information lives: the Fisher falls to zero on relaxation}

Resolving the per-step information in time (Figure 3--figure supplement 1) yields two results. The first closes the mechanism, and it turns on a change of sign. Per step the score varies \emph{less} than the information predicts: the median $\log_{10}(J_t/F_t)$ over the informative steps is about $-0.18$ for R, $-0.25$ for MR and $-0.15$ for VR, while IR sits near $+0.01$ and is the only rung whose bootstrap interval covers zero at most steps ($64$ to $91\%$ of them, against $0$ to $28\%$ for the other three). % src: Figure_3_supplement_1_caption.md
Accumulated over the recording the same ratio rises \emph{above} one, to $1.19$ for MR and $1.20$ for VR against $1.08$ for IR. % src: Figure_3_caption.md
A discrepancy that reverses sign between the per-step and the accumulated statistic cannot be a per-sample modelling error. It is what a positively correlated score does, and the correlation is the one Figure 3 shows directly. Every rung gets each interval nearly right; they differ in how the intervals combine.

The second result is a property of the measurement, not of any one filter. For every rung the information about the channel number $N_{\mathrm{ch}}$, the opening rate $k_{\mathrm{on}}$ and the unitary current $i$ drops to zero the moment the agonist is removed and the open population relaxes: having conditioned on the past, the filter gains nothing further from watching the channels decay. % src: Figure_3_supplement_1_caption.md
The closing rate $k_{\mathrm{off}}$ is the exception, staying informative through the relaxation, which its own kinetics govern. Because this behaviour belongs to the macroscopic observable and not to the approximation, it survives every member of the family, and it answers directly the question of when each parameter stops being measured that a recent treatment of this class of models leaves open \cite{delcore2025parameter}.

\subsection*{A validity map across channels, interval, and noise}

The recovery and time-resolved panels fix one operating point; the deliverable is the whole design space. Figure 4 places the literature's recursive filter R against the interval-conditioned filter IR over the plane of channel number, acquisition interval and instrumental noise, in both moments of the estimate. IR is itself an approximation and must fail somewhere, so where its own rows depart from calibration is where the paper locates that failure.

More channels do not rescue R. Its $k_{\mathrm{off}}$ information distortion sits on a flat floor, with median over the seven intervals of $1.37$, $1.44$, $1.44$ and $1.42$ across $N_{\mathrm{ch}}$ $10$, $100$, $1000$ and $10000$, while IR converges to one ($1.32$, $1.10$, $1.00$, $1.00$); its $N_{\mathrm{ch}}$ direction crosses from conservative to over-confident, $0.87$, $1.10$, $1.23$, $1.26$, and keeps growing, while IR converges. % src: Figure_4_caption.md
The two agree at the fewest channels and diverge from there, so the $10^4$ column is the most informative, not the emptiest.
R also carries a first-moment cost IR does not: a flat bias in the channel number of about $0.13$ to $0.15$ in $\log_{10}$ units, close to a $40\%$ error that does not shrink with channel count, while IR's bias is at or near zero everywhere and both rungs' $k_{\mathrm{off}}$ bias is near zero. % src: Figure_4_caption.md
And the distortion is diffuse for R, confined for IR: the fraction of the plane departing by at least $15\%$ reaches $0.81$ for R's $k_{\mathrm{off}}$ and $0.71$ for its $N_{\mathrm{ch}}$ at $10^4$ channels, against zero for IR in both. % src: Figure_4_caption.md
Conditioning localises the distortion; it does not merely shrink it.

Instrumental noise cures R only at few channels. Its $k_{\mathrm{off}}$ distortion at the higher noise level starts near one where the instrumental noise dominates the small-$N_{\mathrm{ch}}$ gating variance, then re-emerges toward $1.2$ to $1.4$ as $N_{\mathrm{ch}}$ grows, because the gating variance it distorts grows with the channels. % src: Figure_4_caption.md
There is no channel count at which more instrumental noise leaves R calibrated, which is the quantitative form of the boundary between the gating-dominated and instrument-dominated regimes.

Where IR does depart from calibration, at the fewest channels, it departs in the conservative direction: it under-states rather than over-states what the data determine, which is the harmless direction for a user to be wrong in. The window-ignoring approximation fails the other way, inflating the information the data carry. This is the one asymmetry a practitioner needs, and it runs in IR's favour. One cell is carried openly as pathological: at $N_{\mathrm{ch}}=10^4$ and $\Delta\!\cdot\!k_{\mathrm{off}}=1$ the $N_{\mathrm{ch}}$ direction is not identified for either algorithm (the Gaussian Fisher covariance is ill-conditioned there), so a displacement that costs almost no likelihood is amplified into a large parameter shift for R, whose reported bias reads about $-2.8$ in $\log_{10}$ and whose distortion falls off scale; those cells are drawn grey, their sign real and their magnitude not. % src: Figure_4_caption.md

\subsection*{Decomposing the distortion: correlation versus sample}

The shape of the map has a mechanism. The information distortion separates into two parts, one from the correlation of the signal across intervals and one from the non-Gaussian shape of a single interval's averaged signal, the sample term, and the two failure modes live in different corners (Figure 4--figure supplements 3 and 4). The separation is exact only for the information volume: the log-determinant of the distortion is the sum of a correlation log-determinant and a sample log-determinant, with no residual, while the full matrix combines the two through a two-sided congruence so the trace and eigenvalue pieces carry a residual. % src: correction_idm_reconstruction.md (exact identity IDM = K CDM K^T, K = H^{-1/2} J_s^{1/2}; only the log-det composes)
This is what explains R's flat floor: its sample part goes to one with the channel count exactly as IR's does, the per-sample Gaussian approximation becoming exact for both, while its correlation part stays elevated and rises with $N_{\mathrm{ch}}$, from about $1.35$ to $1.47$ on $k_{\mathrm{off}}$. % src: decisions.md#L194-199
More channels push the sample term down and the correlation term up by the same amount, leaving the total flat: R and IR do not differ in per-sample fidelity, they differ in temporal decorrelation, which is what conditioning on the interval buys.

The sample term behaves in a way worth stating on its own. It is non-monotonic in the channel number: the single-interval non-Gaussianity falls as channels are added and the central-limit theorem takes hold, while the gating fraction of the signal variance rises, and the product of the two opposing trends leaves a worst channel count in between. Instrumental noise adds a pure-variance pedestal that dilutes the single-interval non-Gaussianity, so more noise makes the per-sample Gaussian approximation better. % src: sample_correlation_distortion_analysis.md
We report this as a measurement; we do not present the fitted scaling as a confirmed law. One feature of the map is easy to misread and belongs in the text rather than a caption: the large correlation distortion at the shortest intervals is the correct cost of oversampling. When the acquisition interval is far shorter than the channel's correlation time, successive samples carry redundant information, and a faithful likelihood has to register that redundancy, so the left edge of the map shows the theory working as intended.

\subsection*{The design trade-off}

The recovery and calibration panels establish which likelihood to use and that its reported uncertainty is honest; the last figure uses that honesty to advise the experiment (Figure 5). Only IR is shown, because only a calibrated algorithm's reported covariance can guide design, and its distortion-corrected covariance is the precision the experiment actually achieves rather than an optimistic claim.

The reading that gives an experimenter something to change is that the rates and the amplitude directions give opposite advice. At a fixed total channel budget, the pooled variance of the closing rate $k_{\mathrm{off}}$ is nearly flat across channel count (it runs $0.32$, $0.38$, $0.40$ and $0.40$ across $N_{\mathrm{ch}}$ $10$ to $10^4$, a factor of $1.24$ end to end), and the opening rate is similar (a factor of $3.1$), so their information is close to linear in channels and concentrating the budget into few large recordings costs almost nothing. % src: Figure_5_caption.md
The amplitude, count and noise directions rise steeply over the same range, by a factor of $288$ for the unitary current, $60$ for the channel number and $3200$ for the instrumental-noise level, so for these directions the same budget spent as many small recordings gives a better estimate. % src: Figure_5_caption.md
How channels are apportioned across patches therefore trades directly against which parameters can be recovered.

Two further readings are practical. A noisier rig penalises few channels: the closing rate stays flat until the highest noise, where at few channels its pooled variance rises (to $0.81$ at $N_{\mathrm{ch}}=10$ against $0.32$ at the low-noise level), because there the instrumental noise is large next to the small-$N_{\mathrm{ch}}$ gating variance. % src: Figure_5_caption.md
And the interval trade-off runs the same way in every panel: the pooled variance is smallest at short intervals and grows toward the coarse end, mildly for the rates and more for the amplitude directions, so a short acquisition interval is the better design for precision, consistent with the calibration trade-off of Figure 4. The per-recording precision behind these numbers, which orders the opposite way because a larger patch always measures better before pooling, is in Figure 5--figure supplement 1.
