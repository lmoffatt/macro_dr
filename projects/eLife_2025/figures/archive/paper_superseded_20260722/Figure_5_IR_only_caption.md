# Figure 5 caption

**Figure 5. Where the interval-conditioned likelihood stops being faithful, in both moments of the estimate.**

MacroIR is an approximation, so it must fail somewhere, and a validation machinery that never finds its own method wanting has not been tested. This figure locates that failure over the design plane and separates its two forms: a shift of the estimate, and a misstatement of the estimate's uncertainty.

The plane is the same throughout. Columns are the channel number N_ch. Within a panel the horizontal axis is the acquisition interval in units of the closing time, Δ·k_off, over the seven values swept inside every simulated cell, and the vertical axis is the instrumental noise, given as the dimensionless label used in the run (the physical Current_Noise is that label divided by 1000). The three circled markers are the design points of (A), each drawn in the column it belongs to. Colour in (B) and (C) is shrunk to the bound of the 95% bootstrap interval nearest the null value, so a cell whose interval covers the null is drawn white and **every coloured departure is significant at 95%**, with the value shown being the conservative bound rather than the point estimate.

**(A) Grouped MLE distributions at three design points: what the algorithm reports, and what the estimates do.** Each grey point is one maximum-likelihood estimate (MLE) of log10 k_off and log10 N_ch, fitted jointly to a group of 100 simulated recordings. "Grouped" names that pooling, and the group size matters here because it governs how close the estimator sits to its asymptotic normal limit. Three 95% ellipses are drawn at the cloud mean: the empirical covariance of the estimates (grey), the covariance the algorithm reports from its own Gaussian Fisher information (orange, dashed), and the distortion-corrected sandwich covariance (blue, dotted). Three markers carry the first moment: the cross is the true value, the filled circle the mean of the estimates, and the open circle the truth plus the bias the distortion theory predicts, so the two circles coinciding means the theory predicts the shift it finds. Each panel is windowed symmetrically about the truth, so a displacement reads as a displacement, and the pair (k_off, N_ch) is shown because these are the two directions whose distortions carry opposite signs, which makes the reported ellipse the wrong shape and not merely the wrong size.

The two orange numbers are per-axis ratios of variance, empirical over reported, one for each parameter. They are variances, to match the colour scale of (C); the eye measures an ellipse in standard deviations, so the visible width ratio is their square root, and a variance ratio of 1.3 is a reported interval about 14% too narrow. A ratio whose own interval covers one is printed in grey rather than in the scale's colour, because the neutral band of that scale is white and because a number that cannot be distinguished from one should not look like a finding. The three points read: **1, distorted**, where the second moment is wrong and the first is not; **2, biased**, where the estimate is displaced and the reported uncertainty is nearly right; and **3, faithful**, where neither fails.

One limitation of this panel is worth stating plainly. The ellipse compares the covariance of an estimate against the reported covariance, which is the sandwich marginalised over the other parameters, whereas (C) compares the score covariance against the Fisher information without marginalising and without fitting anything. They are not the same quantity and the ellipse is systematically the milder of the two. With 100 fits, moreover, a variance carries about 14% of relative uncertainty on its own. At point 1 the ratio in the N_ch direction reads 1.13 while (C) reports 0.851 for the same cell: at this number of fits the ellipse does not resolve that direction, and the claim about it rests on (C).

**(B) The distortion-induced bias**, the shift the distortion theory predicts for each parameter, in log10 units (a bias of 0.03 is a factor of 10^0.03, that is a 7% error; the largest value here, 0.064, is 16%). It is evaluated at the true parameter, because a bias evaluated at the optimum is zero by construction. It is largest at the **coarse** end of the interval axis and at few channels, and it falls away quickly: along the low-noise line at N_ch = 10 the bias in the N_ch direction goes from 0.064 at Δ·k_off = 1 to 0.056, 0.019, 0.010, 0.008, 0.001 and 0.002 as the interval shortens to 0.01, with k_off following the same order at about a third the size. The fraction of the plane where the bootstrap interval excludes zero falls from 0.55 at N_ch = 10 to 0.38, 0.21 and 0.071 at 20, 50 and 100 for k_off, and comparably for N_ch.

**(C) The information distortion**, the ratio between the variance the score actually has and the variance the algorithm's Gaussian Fisher information claims, evaluated at the pooled optimum. Above one (red) the reported interval is too narrow, below one (blue) too wide. Unlike the bias, it is worst at the **short** end of the interval axis, and its dependence on the interval is a U rather than a ramp: along the same line the k_off distortion reads 1.46 at Δ·k_off = 1, falls to a minimum of 1.21 at 0.5, then climbs to 1.27, 1.40, 1.47, 1.56 and 1.57 as the interval shortens to 0.01. The acquisition interval therefore has an optimum rather than a direction of improvement. The two parameter rows carry **opposite signs**, the closing rate over-confident where the channel number is conservative and by comparable amounts, so any scalar that averages over parameter directions cancels them and reports a faithful algorithm. The failure is visible only per direction and with its sign. Both rows fade with channel number: the fraction of the plane departing by at least 15% falls from 0.48 to 0.33, 0.17 and 0.14 for k_off at N_ch 10, 20, 50 and 100, and reaches zero at 1000.

**The two moments fail at opposite ends of the same axis.** Read (B) and (C) together and the red in (B) hugs the right-hand edge of each panel while the red in (C) hugs the left, with reference points 1 and 2 sitting one in each corner of the same column. No single acquisition interval optimises both: shortening the interval reduces the shift and degrades the reported uncertainty, and lengthening it does the reverse. Choosing an interval is a choice about which moment matters for the question being asked, and that is the practical content of this figure.

Two things this figure does not show. The residual-level diagnostics see none of it: over the same corner the standardized residual variance departs from one by at most 5.5% and its integrated autocorrelation by at most 4.8%, so a user checking only residuals would find this algorithm faithful where its parameter intervals are 20% too narrow. And the failure is not bracketed on two sides, since both quantities are still growing at the lowest noise and the smallest channel number sampled here, so the boundaries drawn at the left and bottom edges of the N_ch = 10 panel are lower bounds on the affected region rather than its edge.
