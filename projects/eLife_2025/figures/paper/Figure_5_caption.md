# Figure 5 caption

**Figure 5. What the interval-conditioned likelihood gets wrong, where on the design plane it does so, and which part of the distortion is responsible.**

MacroIR is an approximation, so it must fail somewhere. This figure locates that failure over the design plane, attributes it, and translates it into the units a reader uses. Each column is a channel number N_ch; within a panel the horizontal axis is the acquisition interval in units of the closing time, Δ·k_off, over the seven swept values, and the vertical axis is the instrumental noise level (the dimensionless label; the physical Current_Noise is the label divided by 1000). Colour is the information distortion, the ratio between the variance the score actually has and the variance the algorithm's own Gaussian Fisher information claims, evaluated at the pooled optimum. A ratio above one (red) means the reported confidence interval is too narrow, below one (blue) that it is too wide. The colour is shrunk to the bound of the 95% bootstrap interval nearest one, so a cell whose interval brackets one is drawn white and every coloured departure is significant at 95%. The solid and dashed contours mark 15% and 50% departures.

**(A) Three design points: what the algorithm reports, and what the estimates do.** Maximum-likelihood estimates of log10 k_off and log10 N_ch (grey points, one per fit, **100 recordings per fit**), with two 95% ellipses drawn at the cloud mean: the empirical covariance of the estimates (grey) and the covariance the algorithm reports from its Gaussian Fisher information (orange, dashed). **The two orange numbers are per-axis ratios of variance**, one for each parameter, not one ratio of areas. They are variances to match the colour scale of the maps below and the area ratio of Figure 2; the eye measures an ellipse in standard deviations, so the visible width ratio is their square root (1.34 in variance is 16% too narrow in width). An area multiplies an inflated direction by a deflated one and hides both, which is precisely the cancellation panel B exists to expose, so a single number here would invite the reading the rest of the figure refutes. Three markers carry the first moment, as in Figure 2: the cross is the true value, the filled circle is the mean of the estimates, and the open circle is the truth plus the bias the distortion theory predicts, so the two circles coinciding means the theory predicts the bias it finds. Each panel is windowed symmetrically about the truth, so a displacement reads as a displacement.

**Two ellipses and not three.** The panel asks one question, whether the reported uncertainty matches the real one, and the sandwich-corrected ellipse answers a different one that Figure 2 already answers for four algorithms. It coincides with the empirical ellipse at two of these three points, so drawing it would add ink without adding a claim; its area ratio is 1.22, 0.82 and 0.88 at the three points. The pair is (k_off, N_ch) because those are the two directions whose distortions have opposite signs, so the reported ellipse is the wrong **shape** and not merely the wrong size.

**Point 2 is a control, not an illustration.** At a failing design point two things act at once: the distortion of the algorithm, which is the subject of this paper, and the estimator's own finite-sample departure from normality, which at ten channels does not vanish even at a hundred recordings per fit and belongs to the group size rather than to the algorithm. Comparing against a distant calibrated cell would not separate them, because it also changes N_ch and with it that finite-sample term. Point 2 therefore holds the channel number, the noise and the group size fixed and moves only the acquisition interval: **the difference between points 1 and 2 is the algorithm alone.** Point 3 is a design point calibrated in every direction, for scale.

At point 1 the reported ellipse is visibly turned relative to the one the estimates occupy, narrow along k_off where the map says the algorithm is over-confident and wide along N_ch where it says the algorithm is conservative: the per-axis ratios read **1.34× and 0.83×**. At point 2, the same cell at its best interval, they close to 1.12× and 0.81× and the ellipses nearly coincide; at point 3 they are 0.91× and 0.90×. The ratios track the per-direction values of the map in sign and in order (1.62 and 0.65 at point 1, 1.25 and 0.91 at point 2, 1.01 and 0.99 at point 3) while sitting closer to one, for two reasons that belong in the reading rather than in the result: the covariance of an estimate is the sandwich marginalised over the other parameters, not the distortion matrix diagonal, and each variance here is estimated from 100 fits and so carries about 14% of relative uncertainty on its own.

The first moment reads off the two circles. At point 1 they sit almost on top of each other and slightly off the cross, so the small bias present is the bias the theory predicts. At point 2 the estimates are displaced from the truth by more than at point 1 and the prediction follows them, which is worth noticing: **the best interval for the variance is not the best interval for the bias**. At point 3 all three markers coincide.

A note on the group size, which is 100 here and not 10. At 10 the estimator carries a finite-sample variance inflation of its own: on point 1's cell the empirical/Fisher area ratio is 2.22 at group 10 against 1.15 at group 100 for the coarsest interval, 1.33 against 0.90 at the best interval, and 1.55 against 1.21 at the finest. Excess kurtosis is small at both group sizes (−0.2 to 0.8), so this is an O(1/n) variance term rather than heavy tails. **Panels (B) and (C) are untouched by any of this**: they compare the score covariance with the Fisher information and fit no estimates at all.

**(B) The same comparison over the whole plane, in every parameter direction.** The circled markers are the design points of (A), each in the column it belongs to, so the concrete ellipse and the abstract surface are the same fact seen twice. All five directions are shown because the point of the panel is what they do jointly. The two kinetic directions inflate, k_off worst (up to 1.57) and k_on the same way but weaker (up to 1.24); the channel-number direction **deflates**, down to 0.65; and the unitary current and the noise amplitude stay null, never departing by 15% anywhere in the volume. Over the 24 cells and 7 intervals the fraction of points departing by at least 15% is 15% for k_off, 6% for N_ch, 3.6% for k_on and 0% for both i and σ_noise. Because the departures carry opposite signs and comparable size, **any scalar that averages over parameter directions cancels them and reports a calibrated algorithm**: the failure is visible only per direction and with its sign.

IR is calibrated over most of the plane and fails in two places: the telegraphic corner, where the interval becomes short compared with the relaxation, and a second, narrower edge at the coarsest interval. At N_ch = 10 and noise 0.05 the k_off distortion reads 1.46 at Δ·k_off = 1, falls to a minimum of 1.21 at 0.5, then rises again to 1.27, 1.40, 1.47, 1.56 and 1.57 as the interval shortens to 0.01. **The dependence on the interval is a U, not a ramp**, so the acquisition interval has an optimum rather than a direction of improvement. Both failures fade as the channel number and the noise rise: at N_ch = 10,000 the whole plane is calibrated.

**(C) The same distortion split into its two parts**, on k_off, the direction that carries it. The sample part is the distortion a per-interval non-Gaussianity would produce; the correlation part is what remains once that is divided out, the residual correlation between intervals that the interval closure fails to remove. At point 1 the correlation part is 1.52 (95% CI 1.47 to 1.57) and the sample part is 1.06 (1.054 to 1.061): the failure is **eight to one a correlation failure**. The two also behave differently along the interval axis, the sample part rising to a bounded hump and decaying again as the interval shortens, the correlation part rising monotonically into the corner. The telegraphic failure is therefore a failure to decorrelate successive intervals, not a mis-modelled single interval, and not an implementation artefact. The channel-number direction is correlation-led as well (0.68 against 0.94 at point 1), so its conservatism has the same origin and the opposite sign.

The safe region, at a 15% criterion, is N_ch above roughly 500, **or** noise above roughly 0.5, **or** an interval between 0.2 and 1 in units of the closing time. Two remarks on what this figure does not show. The residual-level diagnostics see none of it: over the same corner the standardized residual variance departs from one by at most 5.5% and its integrated autocorrelation by at most 4.8%, so a user checking only residuals would find this algorithm calibrated where its parameter intervals are 20% too narrow. And the failure is not bracketed on two sides: the correlation part is still growing at the lowest noise and the smallest channel number sampled here, so the boundary drawn at the left and bottom edges of the N_ch = 10 panel is a lower bound on the affected region, not its edge.
