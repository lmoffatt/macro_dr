# Figure S2 caption

**Figure S2. The log-likelihood of a single recording measures how atypical that realization was, not how well its parameters were recovered.**

The cloud of maximum-likelihood estimates of Figure 2, with each estimate coloured by the maximized log-likelihood (logL) of its own recording. Two parameter pairs are shown, one per row: the kinetic pair (log10 k_on, log10 k_off) and the amplitude pair (log10 N_ch, log10 i); the columns are the five approximations NR, MNR (Mean Non-Recursive), R, MR and IR. The truth is the cross. The colour is logL on a viridis scale rescaled within each algorithm to its own 2nd-to-98th percentile range, so that variation inside a single cloud is visible (high logL is yellow, low logL is purple); the between-algorithm difference in logL, which is large, is therefore removed by construction and is not what this figure shows.

One would expect the highest-logL recordings (yellow) to sit near the truth and the lowest (purple) at the cloud edges. They do not. In the kinetic row the colour is unstructured: the number in each panel is the Spearman correlation between logL and the Mahalanobis distance of the estimate from the cloud mean, and it is approximately zero for every algorithm, so the low-logL recordings are not the mis-recovered ones. In the amplitude row the colour instead runs as a smooth gradient along the N_ch·i degeneracy ridge (higher N_ch and lower i give higher logL), which is a property of the likelihood geometry along the poorly identified direction, not of recovery quality; the ellipse of Figure 2 already normalizes that direction away, which is why the correlation with recovery distance stays near zero.

The reason is that the per-recording logL is, up to a term that is nearly the same for all recordings, minus one half of the sum of squared standardized residuals of that recording. It therefore measures the magnitude of the residuals, that is, how atypical this particular draw of measurement noise and stochastic channel-gating trajectory happened to be. Where the estimate lands, in contrast, is set by the direction of the residuals. Magnitude and direction are independent, so two recordings of the same true parameters can have very different log-likelihoods and both recover the parameters equally well. A high likelihood flags a typical, low-noise realization, not a good estimate.

The recording cell and estimation are those of Figure 2.
