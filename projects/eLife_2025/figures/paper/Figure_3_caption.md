# Figure 3 caption

**Figure 3. The calibration of the filter over time, from its output through the residual to the accumulated score, across the five approximations.**

One common set of 1,000 simulated recordings (a patch of N_ch = 100 two-state, closed ⇌ open, channels, instrumental noise 1e-4, measurement interval 0.1 τ with τ the model's characteristic time) is scored by each of the five likelihood approximations: NR (Non-Recursive), MNR (Mean Non-Recursive), R (Recursive), MR (Mean Recursive) and IR (Interval Recursive). Each row is a time-resolved test of one calibration property, and the columns are the five approximations. Unless stated otherwise the quantity is a mean over the 1,000 recordings, the shaded band or vertical bar is its 95% interval (1.96 standard errors), and the green line is the value a calibrated filter would give. The grey band marks the agonist pulse. Time is in milliseconds.

(A) The output. One example recording: the predicted current (blue, mean ± one standard deviation) over the observed current (grey points), on a scale shared by the five columns. The number in each column is the ensemble-mean total log-likelihood ± its standard error over the 1,000 recordings, the quantity the filter maximizes. The recursive trio reaches about −145 and the naive pair about −230, a gap of roughly 87 nats: the data are about e^87 times more probable under the recursive, conditional filters. (B) Residual variance: the mean squared standardized residual, r² with r = (observed current − predicted mean) / predicted standard deviation, which is one for a calibrated filter (the data-level second moment, on a log10 axis). (C) Score bias for k_off: the mean per-interval score s_t = ∂ logL_t / ∂ k_off, which is zero when the score is unbiased (the inference-level first moment). (D) Per-interval information ratio: Var(s_t) / F_t, with F_t the per-step Fisher information, which is one when the score variance matches the information at each interval; it holds for all five, so the local information content is calibrated. (E) Accumulated information ratio: Var(Σ s_t) / Σ F_t = J_T / F_T, which is one when the same equality holds over the whole recording (log10 axis). The naive pair diverges from one while the recursive trio stays near it; this accumulated ratio is the overconfidence factor of Figure 2. (F) Score autocorrelation for k_off against lag, zero for an uncorrelated score. The temporal correlation of the score is why the per-interval identity (D, near one) fails once the score is summed (E, diverges): a correlated score makes the variance of the sum exceed the sum of the per-step informations.

Rows B, C and E use a separate scale for each of two blocks, the non-recursive pair (NR, MNR) and the recursive trio (R, MR, IR), because the non-recursive departures would otherwise flatten the recursive trio, which sits near the calibrated line; the y-axis numbers therefore appear once per block, on NR and on R, and row D is on one shared scale. Rows A to E are resolved in time and carry the agonist shading; row F is resolved in lag. The score is shown for k_off as a representative parameter, being essentially parameter-independent; all four parameters appear in the figure supplement.
