# Figure 4 caption

**Figure 4. Where in time each parameter is measured, and the per-step calibration of that information, across the five approximations.**

For the same 1,000 recordings as Figure 3 (a patch of N_ch = 100 two-state channels, instrumental noise 1e-4, measurement interval 0.1 τ), the per-step information about each parameter is resolved in time. Rows come in pairs, one pair per parameter (the opening and closing rates k_on and k_off, the unitary current i, and the channel number N_ch); the columns are the five approximations NR (Non-Recursive), MNR (Mean Non-Recursive), R (Recursive), MR (Mean Recursive) and IR (Interval Recursive). The grey band marks the agonist pulse and time is in milliseconds.

The taller row of each pair is the information profile, on a log10 axis: the expected per-step Fisher information F_t = E[I_t] (blue), computed as I_t = (∂ y_mean/∂θ)² / y_var + ½ (∂ y_var/∂θ)² / y_var² from the predictive mean y_mean and variance y_var of the interval, and the variance of the per-step score J_t = Var(s_t) over the recordings (orange). The two coincide wherever the filter is calibrated. The shorter row is their per-step ratio log10(J_t / F_t), on a linear axis with a green line at zero (the calibrated value); the shaded band is a 90% bootstrap interval over the recordings, and a point is drawn only where the parameter carries information (F_t above a small floor).

The information profiles show when each parameter is measured. For the recursive filters (R, MR, IR) the information about k_on, i and N_ch drops to zero the moment the agonist is removed: those filters have already extracted it during the pulse and, conditioning on the past, gain nothing further from watching the channels relax. The closing rate k_off is the exception, staying informative through the decay, which the closing kinetics govern, and the unitary current keeps a conductance-noise floor throughout. The non-recursive filters (NR, MNR) instead keep drawing information after the pulse, at a declining rate, because each interval is scored afresh from the same fixed start. This is the analytic signature of the conditional filter: it registers when it has finished measuring a quantity.

The ratio rows show that the per-step identity J_t = F_t holds for all five approximations (the ratio sits on zero within the bootstrap interval). The large mismatch between the score variance and the Fisher information of the naive methods, the overconfidence of Figure 2, therefore comes entirely from the cross-time correlation of the score (Figure 3F); the per-step magnitudes agree.
